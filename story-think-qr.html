Who do you think-QR?

From BritPop to Brexit: A brief history of the Quick Response code

By Paul Fillingham

I have an earworm that harks back to the nineteen seventies. It's 'Who do you think you are?" by Candlewick Green. The song has been there for some days now, conjuring up visions of schoolboy fishing trips to Newark-on-Trent and late-night sketching sessions inspired by Marvel superhero comics.

Canadian Hughie Green hosted the talent show Opportunity Knocks in the 1960's and 70's. His catchphrases are as persistent as the musical earworms.

An earworm is a form of involuntary cognition that can lead to repetitive memory flashbacks. It's not much of an leap to go from 'Who do you think you are?' to 'Who do you think-QR?'—a wordplay on 'think' and the acronym 'QR' (Quick Response).

As a consequence, I acquired an eclectic taste in music and enough earworms to last a lifetime.

Paul Fillingham

Cycles of Culture

I hadn't really thought about QR-codes for a long time, but since Covid-19 they seem to be everywhere. Culture comes in cycles; the sudden reappearance of the QR-code phenomenon comes with its own backing track originally performed in 1974 by Candlewick Green.

Sir Tim Berners Lee's NeXT computer workstation at CERN.

1994: A Pivotal Year

The QR barcode format was introduced by Japanese auto-parts manufacturer Denso Wave in 1994. In the same year, Sir Tim Berners-Lee established the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) at MIT.

Geri Halliwell at the Brit Awards in 1997.

The Great Reappearance

Despite early magic, scan-rates were initially low. However, in 2020, contactless technologies were suddenly propelled into the mainstream in response to the pandemic.

A new feature introduced in 2021 allows Google Chrome users to generate QR-codes directly from the address bar.

We are so pleased to see the return of QR-codes that we're playing 'Who do you think you are?' non-stop in our studio.
