Who do you think-QR?

From BritPop to Brexit: A brief history of the Quick Response code

By Paul Fillingham

I have an earworm that harks back to the nineteen seventies[cite: 6]. It's 'Who do you think you are?" by Candlewick Green[cite: 7]. The song has been there for some days now, conjuring up visions of schoolboy fishing trips to Newark-on-Trent and late-night sketching sessions inspired by Marvel superhero comics; The Avengers, Spiderman, The Incredible Hulk, and my concession to DC comics The Green Lantern because I loved Gil Kane's artwork[cite: 7, 8].

Canadian Hughie Green hosted the talent show Opportunity Knocks in the 1960's and 70's[cite: 31]. His catchphrases are as persistent as the musical earworms[cite: 32].

I'd work late into the night, listening to the radio[cite: 9]. National stations like Radio One were inaccessible due to interference from the village colliery, but I was able to tune into 'Fab 208'. Radio Luxemburg (208 medium wave) had a rolling playlist of glam-rock, pop and soul[cite: 11]. As a consequence, I acquired an eclectic taste in music and enough earworms to last a lifetime[cite: 12].

In case you are not familiar with the concept, an earworm is a form of involuntary cognition that can lead to repetitive memory flashbacks[cite: 13].

It's not much of an leap to go from 'Who do you think you are?' to 'Who do you think-QR?'—a wordplay on 'think' and the acronym 'QR' or Quick Response (code) which happens to be the subject of this blog post[cite: 14, 15]. I hadn't really thought about QR-codes for a long time, but since Covid 19 they seem to be everywhere[cite: 16].

Cycles of Culture

Culture is like that, it comes in cycles and has lots of surprising random connections[cite: 17]. The sudden reappearance of the QR-code phenomenon comes its own backing track, and was originally performed in 1974 by Opportunity Knocks talent show winners, Candlewick Green[cite: 18].

1994: A Pivotal Year

The QR barcode format was introduced by Japanese auto-parts manufacturer Denso Wave in 1994[cite: 34]. In the same year, British computer scientist Sir Tim Berners Lee left his cherished web server at CERN to take up his new post at MIT where he established the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C)[cite: 34, 35]. 1994 is also the year music mogul Simon Fuller founded the Spice Girls, leading to a resurgence of teen pop leveraging slogans like 'Cool Britannia'[cite: 39].

Geri Halliwell stormed the Brit Awards ceremony in 1997 wearing her signature union jack dress[cite: 40].

By 2009, QR-codes had started to appear in Sean Clark's community websites including 'Empedia'[cite: 47]. I joined the project team as design lead shortly after it was granted funding from the MLA[cite: 48]. Visitors to Newark Aviation Museum augmented their experience with QR-code triggered media playback[cite: 56].

The Great Reappearance

One of the barriers to adoption was initial resistance by Apple to embed the technology into its iPhone Operating System (iOS) [cite: 108]; a situation remedied in 2017[cite: 109]. At the beginning of 2020, with the world locked-down in response to the Covid-19 pandemic, contactless technologies were suddenly propelled into the mainstream[cite: 123].

A new feature introduced in 2021, allows Google Chrome users to generate QR-codes directly from the browser address bar[cite: 137].

We are so pleased to see the return of QR-codes that we're playing 'Who do you think you are?' non-stop in our studio[cite: 140, 141]. Remember folks, it's your vote that counts[cite: 142]...
