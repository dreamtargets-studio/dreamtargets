Who do you think-QR?

From BritPop to Brexit: A brief history of the Quick Response code

By Paul Fillingham

I have an earworm that harks back to the nineteen seventies. It's 'Who do you think you are?" by Candlewick Green. The song has been there for some days now, conjuring up visions of schoolboy fishing trips to Newark-on-Trent and late-night sketching sessions inspired by Marvel superhero comics.

I'd work late into the night, listening to the radio. National stations like Radio One were inaccessible due to interference from the village colliery, but I was able to tune into 'Fab 208'. Radio Luxemburg had a rolling playlist of glam-rock, pop and soul.

I acquired an eclectic taste in music and enough earworms to last a lifetime.

Paul Fillingham

An earworm is a form of involuntary cognition that can lead to repetitive memory flashbacks. It's not much of an leap to go from 'Who do you think you are?' to 'Who do you think-QR?'—a wordplay on 'think' and the acronym 'QR' (Quick Response).

Cycles of Culture

I hadn't really thought about QR-codes for a long time, but since Covid-19 they seem to be everywhere. Culture is like that; it comes in cycles. The sudden reappearance of the QR-code phenomenon comes with its own backing track, originally performed in 1974 by Opportunity Knocks talent show winners, Candlewick Green.

1994: A Pivotal Year

The QR barcode format was introduced by Japanese auto-parts manufacturer Denso Wave in 1994. In the same year, Sir Tim Berners-Lee established the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) at MIT. It was also the year music mogul Simon Fuller founded the Spice Girls.

Early Adoption & Experiments

By 2009, QR-codes started to appear in Sean Clark's community websites, including 'Empedia'. Visitors to Newark Aviation Museum used QR-code triggered media to augment their experience.

The Great Reappearance

Despite early magic, scan-rates were initially low. However, in 2020, with the world locked-down in response to the pandemic, contactless technologies were suddenly propelled into the mainstream.

We are so pleased to see the return of QR-codes that we're playing 'Who do you think you are?' non-stop in our studio.
