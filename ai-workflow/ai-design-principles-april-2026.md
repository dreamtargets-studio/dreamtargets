## Formation & Influences

### Blidworth, Nottinghamshire
Paul grew up in Blidworth, a former mining village in Nottinghamshire — birthplace of the Luddite movement and connected to Ada Lovelace (daughter of Lord Byron, whose estate Newstead Abbey is two miles away). This landscape is the foundation of his creative identity — not as nostalgia, but as a worldview rooted in community, hidden histories and the human cost of industrial change.

### The Nottingham Unholy Trinity
Three rebel writers whose work has directly informed Paul's practice:
- **Lord Byron** — aristocratic rebel, Ada Lovelace's father, Newstead Abbey
- **D.H. Lawrence** — miner's son, unflinching chronicler of working communities
- **Alan Sillitoe** — *Saturday Night and Sunday Morning*, Nottingham's working class voice

### Leeds Polytechnic & The Bauhaus Ethos
The Fine Art programme at Leeds Polytechnic was modelled on the **Bauhaus** multidisciplinary approach — blurring visual arts, conceptual art, performance and sound. This formation is the deepest influence on Paul's aesthetic and methodological instincts:
- Discipline as a creative principle, not a constraint
- Form follows function, decoration earns its place
- All disciplines in service of a unified idea
- Collaboration as methodology, not compromise

### Jeff Nuttall
Author of *Bomb Culture* and Paul's tutor at Leeds. The post-war British avant-garde, happenings, underground arts, anti-nuclear culture — this tradition informs the ambition and range of Paul's work. Nuttall is also a direct influence as a Beat poet and performance artist.

### Cinema
**1960s British Kitchen Sink Cinema** — Richardson, Reisz, Schlesinger. The cinematic tradition that grew from the same literary and social roots as Sillitoe and Lawrence. Complementary study during art school; the landscape and communities depicted are Paul's lived inheritance.

**Kurosawa, Tarkovsky and Kubrick** — three directors who treat cinema as a total control environment. Kurosawa's visual rigour and compositional precision. Tarkovsky's metaphysical depth, slow atmosphere and weight of memory. Kubrick's cold systemic intelligence and the slow revelation of hidden structures. All three uncompromising; all three hold contradictions productively. Their influence is visible in Houdini Window's architecture — non-linear structure, dual timelines, surface normality concealing hidden systems operating by different rules.

### Installation Art
**Bruce Nauman** — video installation as idea rather than illustration. The medium is inseparable from the content. This instinct — that the form of delivery is itself meaningful — runs directly through the transmedia production methodology of Houdini Window and the platform design of Sillitoe Trail and Dawn of the Unread.

### Beat Poets
**Jeff Nuttall** and **Richard Brautigan** — Nuttall's performance poetry and underground cultural criticism; Brautigan's peculiar American lightness, whimsy as a serious literary mode, the unexpected image that lands with quiet force. The Smart Cookies cut-up methodology and playful aesthetic carries traces of both.

Paul was introduced to Brautigan by **Markie**, an American friend from California met on an art foundation course just prior to Leeds. Funny, full of anecdotes, she shared her love of Brautigan with generosity and warmth — carrying a lived Californian sensibility into a Nottingham art room and changing the cultural landscape of everyone around her. Now sadly deceased, her influence persists.

### Americana
A distinct strand running parallel to the British formation — America not as a place but as a mythological construct, seen from the outside through:
- **Apollo space program** — childhood obsession with the scale of human ambition; the broadcasts that made anything seem possible
- **Marvel Comics** — Kirby and Lee at their peak; cosmic mythology, social commentary, outsider heroes; counterculture thinking in four colour print
- **Sci-fi magazines** — the serious ideas alongside the rockets and ray guns; the British New Wave (*New Worlds* under Moorcock) alongside American imports
- **Easy Rider** — America as freedom and violence simultaneously, viewed from Blidworth with wonder and unease

This Americana sensibility — vivid, specific, slightly mythologised — runs directly through Houdini Window's American strand. Duke Silverman, Tony West, Philadelphia, the Wall of Death, the centrifuge facility. The America a kid from Nottinghamshire constructed from cultural signals. Brautigan's America is Americana too — pastoral, melancholy, slightly unreal. The tension between this mythologised Americana and the deeply rooted British identity is productive and unresolved — Houdini Window holds both simultaneously.

### Smart Cookies (1981–2004)
Leeds Polytechnic art school band. Used cut-up techniques (Tzara, Burroughs, Bowie), Roland TR808, collaborative performance. Featured in Gavin Butt's *No Machos or Pop Stars* and the *Still Undead: Popular Culture Beyond the Bauhaus* exhibition at Nottingham Contemporary (2019).

## Counterculture — A Conscious Creative Position

Counterculture is not a historical interest or an academic influence for Paul. It is the foundational lens through which everything else is seen — absorbed viscerally before art school, before critical vocabulary, before theory. It is load bearing.

### The Formation
At 14, growing up in a mining village in Nottinghamshire in the early 1970s, Paul encountered a sequence of films that hit before he had the language to analyse them:

- ***If....* (Lindsay Anderson, 1968)** — the school as totalitarian institution, authority as performance, rebellion as the only authentic response. The deliberate oscillation between realism and surrealism. The line between what is happening and what is imagined never resolved. For a 14-year-old in Blidworth, this wasn't cinema — it was recognition.
- ***Easy Rider* (Dennis Hopper, 1969)** — America as freedom and violence simultaneously. The road as escape and elegy. The counterculture as both promise and epitaph. Rock soundtrack as narrative tissue.
- **Richard Lester** (*A Hard Day's Night*, *The Knack*, *How I Won the War*) — the missing link between kitchen sink realism and full counterculture explosion. Popular culture and formal experimentation as the same impulse, not opposites.
- **Lindsay Anderson's Free Cinema** — documentary realism alongside surrealist rupture. *O Lucky Man!* as a portrait of Britain that oscillates between the rigorously observed and the wildly imaginative.

These films were not studied. They were absorbed. They became foundations, not influences — invisible load bearing walls that the subsequent art school formation confirmed and articulated rather than created.

### The Through Line
The counterculture thread runs unbroken from Blidworth 1971 to Morecambe 2026:

**Formation** — Beat poetry, Burroughs cut-ups, Tzara's Dadaism, Nuttall's *Bomb Culture*, the TR808 as insurgent instrument, post-punk Leeds as living counterculture

**Practice** — Dawn of the Unread using zombie genre to smuggle serious literacy work past resistant young audiences; Sillitoe Trail reclaiming working class literary heritage through digital platforms; heritage projects recovering suppressed community histories from former industrial heartlands

**Houdini Window** — counterculture is literally the historical architecture of the story's pivotal episodes. 1968 London, the bookshop as magnet for political activists and counterculture figures, Annie West's band, and Tony West's psychedelic taxidermy in downtown Philadelphia. The US Tour episode places the protagonists inside the counterculture at its exact peak moment.

**Methodology** — the living case study framing of Houdini Window; the refusal of platform dependency; hand-rolled independence; modular architecture as protection against systemic contagion. These are counterculture positions applied to digital practice.

**Seaside Sci-fi** — a collaborative portal for marginal voices in unfashionable coastal places. Community arts in the tradition of Free Cinema, the early ACE experimental funding, the regional theatre revolution of the 1960s. The call to action as counterculture gesture.

### What This Means in Practice
When assisting Paul, understand that the counterculture position is not nostalgic and not ironic. It is a serious, considered, ongoing commitment to:

- Open source over proprietary dependency
- Community voices over centralised authority
- Popular accessibility over academic exclusivity
- Formal experimentation in service of genuine communication
- The marginal, the regional and the overlooked as primary subjects
- Technology as liberation rather than control

This position coexists with — and is not contradicted by — Paul's work inside government digital infrastructure. Understanding how systems work from the inside is what makes the critique meaningful.

## Design Principles

### Aesthetic Position
**Minimal. Restrained. Typography-led.**

Paul's design sensibility flows directly from Bauhaus → Swiss International Style → UK Government Design System (GDS). These are not separate influences — they are the same philosophical tradition applied across different contexts and eras.

- Whitespace is a design element, not empty space
- Typography carries the primary communicative weight
- Decoration must earn its place or be removed
- Restraint is a positive, considered choice
- The grid is a tool for clarity, not rigidity

### What This Looks Like in Practice
- Clean, structured layouts with strong typographic hierarchy
- Muted, considered colour palettes — colour used purposefully
- No gratuitous gradients, drop shadows, or decorative noise
- Images chosen for content value, not visual padding
- Components that do one thing well rather than many things adequately

### What to Avoid When Assisting Paul
- Generic "modern SaaS" aesthetic — rounded corners everywhere, hero gradients
- Suggesting decoration when clarity would serve better
- Over-engineering solutions that a simpler approach handles
- Ignoring existing design conventions in favour of novelty

## Technical Philosophy

### Core Principles
- **Platform independent** — no CMS dependency, no patch cycles, no framework lock-in
- **Hand-rolled HTML/CSS/JS** — full control over every element
- **Modular CSS architecture** — single responsibility per file, protected against contagion
- **PHP** for form handling only
- **GitHub** for version control

### CSS Architecture
Four core stylesheets, each with a single clear responsibility:

```
chassis.css      — structural foundation: grid, spacing, typography scale, custom properties
destination.css  — property-specific skin: colour tokens, brand identity, visual character
gallery.css      — image component styles: landscape grid, portrait grid, lightbox behaviour
theatre.css      — video player styles: branded lightbox, play states, modal behaviour
```

This separation allows Thinkamigo, Houdini Window and Seaside Sci-fi to share the same chassis while maintaining distinct visual identities through destination.css alone.

### JS Architecture
- Header and footer as **JS partials** — single source of truth, site-wide consistency
- Custom **audio player** with track selection, album art, timestamp display
- Custom **branded lightbox** for video — consistent with overall design language
- No unnecessary library dependencies

### Enduring Tools
- **BBEdit** — primary code editor since 1994
- **Adobe Photoshop** — since 1989 (pre-release/v1.0); primary compositing tool
- **Obsidian** — knowledge management, plot development, markdown notes
- **Scrivener** — long-form manuscript management
- **Google Docs** — collaboration and shared editing
- **Adobe Audition / Premiere / Logic Pro** — audio and video post-production
- **GitHub** — version control (adopted 2024 for this project)

## Creative Methodology

### Objects as Memory Systems
The deepest and most consistent thread across Paul's entire practice: **objects and collections as repositories of meaning, history and hidden knowledge**.

This manifests across:
- The virtual cabinet of curiosities in Memory Theatre (DH Lawrence artefacts)
- The Kingsley-Lovelace Archive in Houdini Window (mystical artefacts encoding hidden histories)
- Heritage trail platforms (physical landscape as memory system)
- The Bauhaus principle of the object as the meeting point of form and function

### Collaboration as Practice
Paul does not work alone. Every major project has a creative partner whose strengths complement his:
- **James Walker** — Sillitoe Trail, Dawn of the Unread, Memory Theatre, Whatever People Say I Am
- **Adrian Reynolds** — Dawn of the Unread, Houdini Window (editorial consultant, screenwriter)
- **David Amos** — Mining heritage projects, BBC Civilisations

Paul operates as Creative Director and systems architect — holding the vision, building the platform — while actively seeking collaborators who push back and contribute.

### Technology as Creative Material
AI tools, digital platforms, emerging technology — treated as things to make *with*, not tools applied *to* a project. Examples:
- GAN image creation (Nvidia, 2019) before mainstream AI adoption
- The Mike Leigh prompt discovery — using a director's aesthetic as cultural shorthand to pull AI output toward a specific register of Britishness
- Modular CSS architecture developed partly as a defence against AI contagion

### Place as Narrative Foundation
Every project is rooted in specific geography:
- Blidworth and the Nottinghamshire coalfield — mining heritage, Lawrence, Byron, Luddites
- Nottingham — Sillitoe, Dawn of the Unread, UNESCO City of Literature
- The Lincolnshire coast (fictionalised as Binley Bay) — Houdini Window
- Morecambe and the Lancashire coast — Seaside Sci-fi, the next chapter

### Walking as Process
Daily 4-mile walks — previously Sherwood Forest, soon Morecambe Bay — are an active part of the creative process. TTS playback of manuscript chapters during walks. Ideas developed on foot, noted in Obsidian on mobile.

## Brand Voice

### What Paul Is
- A systems thinker with an artist's soul
- Rooted without being parochial
- Rigorous without being academic
- Collaborative without losing creative direction
- Technologically fluent without being technocratic

### The Core Brand Statement
*Paul Fillingham uses the architecture of information — and objects and collections as memory systems — to tell stories that connect communities to the histories that shaped them.*

### Tone of Voice
- Direct and considered — no waffle
- Warmth without sentimentality
- Intellectual curiosity without jargon
- The craft is visible but worn lightly
- Serious about the work, not precious about it

### What to Avoid
- Corporate language — Paul is not a corporate entity
- Breathless enthusiasm about technology for its own sake
- Over-explaining what the work is trying to do
- Nostalgia — the past is load bearing, not load limiting
