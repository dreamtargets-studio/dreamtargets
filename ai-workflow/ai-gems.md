**GEM — The Unified Knowledge Management Problem** _Captured: April 2026_

**The core insight:** The demographic cliff and the metadata problem are the same problem viewed from different angles. When the eyewitness leaves, they take the tacit knowledge that makes the metadata meaningful. The metadata was always a substitute for the person. A poor substitute.

**The unified theory:** Knowledge management failure crosses every sector simultaneously — nuclear waste burial sites, coal mining archives, heritage collections, government institutional memory, creative practice, corporate systems. The same failure mode playing out at different speeds with different consequences. Nobody has named it as a single unified problem with a single unified discipline as the response.

**The evidence base — sectors Paul has worked inside:**

- Nuclear Decommissioning Authority — buried waste, long-term data provenance
- Coal Authority / Mining Remediation Authority — national photographic archive, useless metadata, mortgage and subsidence decisions riding on data quality
- Heritage — objects without eyewitnesses, volunteers handing over to Trusts
- Government digital — institutional knowledge retiring daily
- Creative practice — design rationale, briefs, decisions undocumented

**The prior art:** MineCraft the Prequel — published NTU Knowledge Exchange project. Format obsolescence, Dublin Core metadata, AI provenance, photographic archive quality. Already in the record. Needs locating and citing properly.

**The vocabulary of the problem:** Noise, duplicated effort, silos, unrealised value, findability, ownership, curation, standards, metadata, single source of truth, dashboards, archiving, formats, quality control, editorial workflow, storage, right channel/media/format, interoperability, obsolescence.

**The translation job:** Git terminology alienates designers — Commit, Push, Blame sound like Dalek commands. The discipline is sound, the language is wrong. A designer-friendly vocabulary for version control and knowledge management doesn't yet exist. Nobody has written it.

**The guide that needs writing:** Not "Git for designers." Not "version control for creatives." Something bigger — how creative practitioners find the signal in the noise. Starting with the problem, not the tool. Using design language throughout. Covering documents as seriously as files. Honest about the learning curve.

**The Enquire Within connection:** This is the methodology's most commercially powerful expression. The knowledge management offer that crosses every sector, speaks to every organisation losing its senior practitioners, and positions Paul as the person who has seen it from the inside more completely than almost anyone.

**The book. The keynote. The thing worth saying loudly when the constraints lift.**

That's a good phrase. The jury doesn't decide in the room where the evidence is presented — they go away, they sit with it, they let it settle before they deliver a verdict.

It's also very Houdini Window as a working method. The non-linear structure, the dual timelines, the objects carrying histories their owners don't recognise — none of that resolves under pressure. It needs the jury room.

And it connects back to Burke and Brautigan and Meades as influences. None of them are in a hurry to arrive at the point. The deliberation is part of the meaning.


de Bono's Six Thinking Hats is essentially a structured deliberation methodology. You're not rushing to the green hat. You let the other five do their work first.

The jury metaphor sits neatly alongside it. Both are about creating the conditions for a good decision rather than forcing one. The seminar room, the jury room, the Obsidian vault, the manuscript left alone for three weeks. Same principle, different contexts.

That's actually a strong thread for a piece somewhere down the line — distance and deliberation as a creative methodology. Not just for writing, but for design thinking, for UX research, for platform development. The pause as a productive act rather than a failure to get on with it.

Which is also, quietly, an argument for why the best AI workflow isn't the fastest one. The brief, the context, the structured conversation — all of it is deliberate rather than reactive. The vibe coding approach that unravelled itself was the opposite of that. Speed without deliberation.

Another Gem, maybe. Or the seed of a future Substack piece that connects the design thinking work at NTU with the Houdini Window production process. The creative methodology piece. That one could travel quite far.
